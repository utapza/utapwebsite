### Day 17 — LinkedIn (thought leadership)

```
The campus wallet vs. the campus app:

Most "campus apps" are built by universities.
They have poor UX, students don't open them, and they get abandoned within two years.

The difference isn't technology. It's adoption mechanics.

A student ID wallet has a built-in daily trigger:
every access gate, every exam hall, every library scan forces the habit.

An ordering or events app doesn't have that.
It competes with every other app for attention.

The right architecture: build on top of the identity layer, not next to it.

If students carry your app because their student card is in it,
everything else becomes a natural extension rather than a cold download.

That's the uTap thesis.
```

### Day 24 — X Thread (why pickup beats delivery on campus)

```
Ordering from a delivery app between lectures is a bad bet.

Here's why 🧵

1/ Your between-lecture window: 45–75 minutes.
   Your actual eating window: 30–40 minutes.
   Delivery estimate: 30–45 minutes.

   The maths only work if the estimate is exact.
   It usually isn't.

2/ Campus delivery has an access problem.
   Drivers often can't enter pedestrian-heavy campus areas.
   They stop at the gate. Call you. Add 10 minutes.

3/ Meanwhile: the café is 3 minutes away.
   On foot.

4/ Pickup-first ordering:
   Place order before your lecture ends.
   Walk to counter.
   Collect with pickup code.
   Total active time: 4 minutes.

5/ The gap isn't convenience.
   It's habit.
   Delivery apps are designed to be easy to open.
   That ease overrides the fact that walking is faster.

Ordering campus food via delivery is a solved problem.
The solution is pickup, not delivery.
```
