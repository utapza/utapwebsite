## Why campus food costs more than you think

Delivery apps like Mr D and Uber Eats make most of their money between your order and your receipt. By the time you've confirmed payment, several invisible charges have been added.

### The anatomy of a delivery fee

On a typical R100 order from a campus café, here's what gets stacked on top:

- **Delivery fee:** R35–R50 depending on distance, surge pricing, and your subscription tier
- **Service fee / platform fee:** R8–R15 — this is charged regardless of delivery distance
- **Tip (optional but expected):** R10–R20 for the driver

That's R53–R85 in fees on top of a R100 order. Not on a R500 order. On a R100 one.

### How it adds up at scale

The fees feel small in isolation. A R15 service fee doesn't sting. But if you're a student ordering food on campus three to five times a week, those fees become a significant monthly expense — one that most students don't consciously track because it's buried in the total.

## How much are South African students wasting per month?

Let's make this concrete with two student archetypes.

### The 3-times-a-week student

Three delivery orders per week × R53 average fee × 4 weeks = **R636 per month** in delivery fees alone.

Over a semester (6 months): **R3,816.**

That's a return flight between Johannesburg and Cape Town. It's two months of groceries. It's a semester's worth of textbooks.

### The 5-times-a-week student

Five delivery orders per week × R53 average fee × 4 weeks = **R1,060 per month** in delivery fees.

Over a full academic year (10 months): **R10,600.**

For context: the 2026 NSFAS living allowance for students not in NSFAS-accredited accommodation is approximately R1,780 per month. Campus food delivery fees alone can consume more than half of that living allowance if you order from a delivery app every weekday.

## When delivery apps make sense (and when they don't)

We want to be honest here — because the answer isn't "never use Mr D."

**✅ Use Mr D or Uber Eats when:**
- You're off campus, ordering from a restaurant that isn't near your location
- It's late at night and campus food options are closed
- You're ordering a large group order where the per-person fee drops significantly
- The specific restaurant you want isn't available for pickup where you are

**❌ Don't use them when:**
- The café is a 3–5 minute walk from your lecture hall
- You have 20 minutes between classes (the driver won't arrive in time anyway)
- You're ordering from a campus vendor that's already available on uTap
- You're on a tight monthly budget and the fees will genuinely hurt

The core problem with delivery apps on campus isn't that they're bad apps. It's that they're designed for cross-city, restaurant-to-home delivery — not for a campus where every vendor is within a 5-minute walk. The model doesn't fit the geography.
