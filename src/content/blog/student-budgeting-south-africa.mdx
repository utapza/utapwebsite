Most university students in South Africa don't run out of money because they spend on the wrong things. They run out because they spend a little too much on the right things — food, transport, toiletries — without tracking where it's going.

By week three, the NSFAS disbursement or bursary payment that felt fine at the start of the month is nearly gone. The next payment is two weeks away. And there's a test on Thursday.

This guide is about preventing that. Not extreme frugality — just a framework that actually works for South African campus life.

---

## Know your actual monthly number

Before any budgeting advice matters, you need to know exactly how much money comes in each month. Not roughly. Exactly.

For most students, this is one of:
- **NSFAS allowance** — living allowance varies by accommodation status. Students not in NSFAS-accredited accommodation receive approximately R1,780/month (2026). Students in accredited accommodation receive a different structure.
- **Bursary living allowance** — varies widely by bursary provider, typically R2,000–R5,000/month
- **Family support** — whatever your family transfers monthly
- **Part-time income** — if you work, include an honest average (not your best month)

Write that number down. Every spending decision you make this month starts there.

## The five spending categories that matter most

### 1. Food (your biggest controllable expense)

For most students, food is the largest discretionary expense — and the one with the most variation. The gap between "ordering from Mr D every day" and "buying groceries and cooking" is significant.

**Rough benchmarks:**
- Delivery app ordering (5x/week): R1,500–R2,500/month in actual total cost (including fees)
- Campus café (walk-up, no fees, 5x/week): R800–R1,400/month
- Mix of groceries + campus ordering (3–4 days cooking): R600–R1,000/month
- Full self-catering from a grocery store: R400–R700/month

The delivery fee issue is worth naming directly: a R75 campus meal costs R75 if you walk up or use uTap. It costs R120–R145 if you order it on Mr D. [Over a month, that gap is R600–R1,000.](/blog/campus-food-delivery-fees-south-africa)

**Practical fix:** Order from campus vendors directly (walk-up or uTap uShop) for weekday meals. Use Checkers Sixty60, Woolworths Dash, or a grocery trip for weekend food. Reserve delivery apps for genuine convenience situations — not default behaviour.

### 2. Transport

If you live on campus or nearby, this may be low. If you commute, it's often the second-largest expense after food.

**Practical fixes:**
- Monthly taxi/bus passes beat per-trip payments for regular routes
- Plan trips in clusters — one trip out on Saturday for groceries, banking, and personal errands rather than separate trips each day
- If you have a bike, use it. The payback period on a secondhand bike vs. regular taxi costs is under two months for most commuters.

### 3. Data / airtime

Data costs are real and often underestimated. Between lectures, WhatsApp, streaming, and assignment research, students can burn through a lot.

**Practical fix:** Most campuses have free Wi-Fi in libraries, study halls, and residences. Identify where your free Wi-Fi zones are and use them for data-heavy tasks (downloads, video, large uploads). Reserve your mobile data for away-from-campus essentials.

### 4. Toiletries and personal care

This is usually a lumpy expense — it doesn't happen every week, but when it does, it hits hard if you haven't planned for it.

**Practical fix:** Budget R150–R300/month as a fixed line item, even in months where you don't spend it. Build up a small buffer so you're not caught short when toothpaste, soap, and deodorant all run out in the same week.

### 5. Social and entertainment

This is the category most budgeting guides tell you to cut. That's bad advice. Social connection is important for your mental health and academic performance.

**Better approach:** Give it a number, not zero. R200–R400/month for social spending is realistic for most students. Once it's spent, it's spent — not "I'll make it up next month."

## A simple framework that works

Rather than a complicated spreadsheet, most students do better with three buckets:

**Bucket 1: Fixed and essential (pay first)**
- Rent (if not included)
- Data bundle or monthly contract
- Transport passes
- Any recurring commitments

**Bucket 2: Variable but necessary (set a weekly limit)**
- Food
- Toiletries

**Bucket 3: Discretionary (what's left)**
- Social
- Entertainment
- Non-essential shopping

The rule: fill Bucket 1 on day one. Set a weekly limit for Bucket 2. Whatever's left after both is Bucket 3 — and when it's gone, it's gone.

## The NSFAS reality

If you're on NSFAS, the disbursement schedule creates its own rhythm. Payment dates don't always align with when your expenses hit. Some practical things:

- **Know your payment date** — NSFAS disbursements don't always hit on the same day each month. Track your last few and anticipate the next.
- **Don't spend the whole first week** — the first week after disbursement is when most overspending happens. Treat the month as 4 weeks, not "a lot now, less later."
- **Build a float** — even R200–R300 kept as a buffer prevents the "no money for 10 days" crash that hits in week four.

## Frequently asked questions

**How much should a South African student spend on food per month?**

It varies significantly by location and lifestyle, but a realistic range is R600–R1,400/month for campus-based students eating mostly on campus. Students who order food via delivery apps regularly spend R1,500–R2,500/month on food once all fees are included. Self-catering from grocery stores brings the cost down to R400–R700/month.

**How do I make NSFAS last longer?**

The most impactful changes are: (1) reduce delivery app use in favour of walk-up or pickup-first campus ordering, (2) buy groceries for weekend meals rather than ordering every day, (3) plan your month's spending in the first two days rather than spending freely at the start. A R200–R300 float kept in reserve prevents the late-month cash shortage most students experience.

**What is the best budgeting app for South African students?**

Capitec's built-in spending categories are one of the most accessible options — free, automatic, and no extra app to download. For more structured tracking, 22seven (by Old Mutual) links to multiple bank accounts and auto-categorises spending. Notion's free student plan lets you build a custom budget tracker if you prefer full control.
