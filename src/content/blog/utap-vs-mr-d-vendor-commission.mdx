If you run a campus café, tuckshop, or food stall, your relationship with delivery apps probably looks something like this: the orders come in, students are happy, and then you check your statement and feel the sting.

Twenty-seven percent. Sometimes thirty-five. Gone before you've paid for ingredients, packaging, or your own time.

This post breaks down exactly what campus vendors keep on every order across different platforms — and what changes when the commission model goes away.

---

## The commission problem on campus

Delivery platforms like Mr D and Uber Eats were designed for a specific use case: connecting restaurants to customers across a city, using a fleet of drivers to bridge the distance. That model has real costs — drivers, insurance, logistics software — and the 25–35% commission funds all of it.

But campus food isn't cross-city delivery. The café is 200 metres from the res block. The vendor and the student are on the same campus. There's no fleet needed.

The problem is that the same commission structure gets applied anyway. Campus vendors pay city-delivery rates for a pickup-adjacent transaction.

Before expenses — ingredients, packaging, staff wages, electricity — the vendor has already lost more than a third of the order value to the platform.

## "But what about the delivery traffic?"

This is the most common pushback from campus vendors considering a switch — and it's a fair one. Delivery platforms do bring exposure. Students discover vendors through the app.

Here's the honest answer: **on campus, that discovery argument is weaker than it sounds.**

Your customers already know where you are. They walk past your storefront every day. They're not discovering you on Mr D — they're using the app because it's convenient when they're in res. The platform isn't driving new customers for a campus vendor the way it might for a new restaurant opening in Sandton.

What the platform is doing is charging you for access to students who already know you exist.
