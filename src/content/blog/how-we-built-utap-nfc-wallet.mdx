The idea behind uTap is embarrassingly simple in retrospect: your student card is an NFC chip in a piece of plastic. Your phone has an NFC chip. Why are these two separate objects?

That observation — made by a student who'd lost their card for the third time in a semester — became the seed of what uTap is today.

This is the story of what we built, the decisions we made, and what we got wrong before we got it right.

---

## The problem we kept coming back to

South African university students carry a physical card that has to be in the right pocket, every day, at every access point. Forget it once and you're locked out of your res. Lose it and you're facing a R150 replacement fee and three days of reduced campus access.

But it's not just about losing the card. The card is also the anchor for a fragmented campus experience:
- One system for building access
- A different system for campus ordering (or no system — just a queue)
- Another app for event tickets
- WhatsApp for everything else

The result is a campus life spread across 4–6 different apps and a piece of plastic, none of which talk to each other.

We wanted to build the thing that should have existed already: one app, anchored to your campus identity, that handles all of it.

---

## The NFC decision

The first major technical decision was: do we try to work with universities to issue digital IDs from scratch, or do we build on top of the physical cards that already exist?

We went with the second approach — and it was the right call.

Universities have already invested in card infrastructure. The readers, the access control systems, the card printing — it's all in place. Asking an institution to rip that out and replace it with a new digital-native system is a multi-year procurement process. It doesn't move at the speed campus students need.

Instead, we scan the existing MIFARE NFC card and store those credentials on the phone. The campus reader doesn't know or care whether it's talking to a plastic card or a phone — it just sees the right credential and grants access.

This meant we could ship something students could use immediately, without waiting for universities to change their infrastructure.

---

## uShop: the second layer

Once students had their card on their phone, we had a daily-habit app. Students open uTap to check access, to show their ID — and that daily habit creates the context for the next layer.

Campus food ordering was the obvious first extension. Students already know where the café is. They just need a better way to order without queuing.

The key design decision in uShop was: pickup-first, not delivery-first. Delivery on campus doesn't make sense. The vendor is 200 metres away. We built around the actual campus geography — order ahead, pay in-app, collect with a code.

The zero-commission model for vendors wasn't a marketing choice. It was a reflection of the economics: we're not running a driver fleet. There's no delivery infrastructure to fund. The 25–35% that platforms charge to cover those costs doesn't apply to pickup. So we don't charge it.

---

## What we got wrong early

**We underestimated the integration complexity.** MIFARE DESFire cards (used by many universities) have encrypted sectors that require cooperation from the institution to fully implement. Our first build handled Classic cards well. DESFire took longer than we expected. We're still expanding card compatibility.

**We assumed students would self-onboard easily.** The NFC scan flow — hold the card against the back of the phone, wait for the read — is simple once you know what to do. But first-time users needed clearer visual guidance. We've revised the onboarding UI twice based on real user testing.

**We built features before users.** Early versions of uTap had more features than early campuses could actually use. A campus with 200 uTap users doesn't need advanced vendor analytics. We've learned to ship what the current scale needs, not what a hypothetical future scale might want.

## Where we are now

uTap is currently live at Nelson Mandela University (NMU), our first pilot campus. We're in the process of onboarding additional campuses for 2026.

The core product — NFC wallet, uShop ordering, event discovery via uGig — is stable and being used daily. The vendor side has active stores with students placing and picking up orders.

The B2B layer — verified student data packages, precision advertising for external brands targeting specific faculties and year levels — is in planning. It's the long-term monetisation engine, but we're not building it before we've built the campus density that makes it valuable.

---

## What we're building toward

The campus wallet is the foundation. It creates daily habit, campus identity, and the trust layer on which everything else can be built.

From that foundation:
- More campuses, more vendor density, more student network effects
- uGig expanding from event discovery to event ticketing and campus club infrastructure
- B2B data products that let brands and institutions reach verified, segmented student populations
- Hardware integrations with campus access systems for institutions that want deeper integration

The ambition isn't to be a South African Mr D. It's to be the operating system for campus life — the app that every student has because their student card lives there, and that becomes the natural place for everything else campus-related to happen.
