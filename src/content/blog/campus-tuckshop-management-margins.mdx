Campus tuckshops operate in one of the strangest micro-markets in South Africa. Your customer base walks past you every day. They know your menu by heart. And somehow, if you're on a delivery platform, you're handing 27–35% of every transaction to a company that does nothing except put your menu on a phone screen.

This guide is for campus food vendors who want to protect their margins, reduce queue chaos, and build a sustainable business.

---

## What actually kills tuckshop margins

### 1. Platform commissions

If you're on Mr D or Uber Eats, the commission alone accounts for R27–R35 on every R100 in sales. On R4,000 in daily orders, that's R1,080–R1,400 leaving your business every single day before you've bought a single ingredient.

Over a month: **R27,000–R35,000 in commission.**

### 2. Order errors and no-shows

Walk-up orders get confused. WhatsApp messages get missed. Students order and don't collect — leaving you holding prepared food you can't resell. Every uncollected order is margin lost: ingredients spent, labour spent, zero income.

### 3. Queue chaos during peak hours

The 12:45–13:15 lunch rush is your most valuable window. If your counter is overwhelmed, students leave. One bad queue experience can cost you a regular customer permanently.

### 4. Untracked cash

If you're still taking cash orders without tracking them, money is almost certainly leaving through gaps — missed transactions, staff errors, petty theft. Not necessarily malicious, but consistent.

---

## How to manage campus orders better

### Step 1: Separate order taking from preparation

The biggest single improvement most tuckshops can make: stop having the same person take orders and prepare food simultaneously.

Designate one device as the order point. Everything flows from confirmed orders. This reduces errors, speeds up preparation, and eliminates the "did you order the chicken or the beef?" confusion.

### Step 2: Use pickup codes for every order

Give every order a unique pickup code. When a student collects, they show the code. No code = no food.

This eliminates queue-jumping, order confusion, and uncollected food sitting under the wrong name for 20 minutes.

### Step 3: Spread your peak load with pre-ordering

The most effective queue management tool is moving orders out of the lunch rush entirely. If students can order at 11:30 for collection at 13:00, your kitchen load spreads across the morning instead of spiking in 30 minutes.

uTap uShop does this automatically — students order ahead, pay in-app, and pick up with a code. You receive orders with timestamps and prepare them ahead of the rush.

### Step 4: Know your actual cost per order

Most campus vendors track revenue. Fewer track cost per order. A rough framework:

- **Food cost:** 25–35% of sale price
- **Labour cost:** 20–30%
- **Overhead:** 10–15%
- **Target net margin:** 20–30%

If you're giving 30% to a platform before covering any of these, the numbers don't work.
